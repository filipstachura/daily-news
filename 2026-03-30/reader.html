Today's reading digest brings together five essential perspectives on building with AI. We start with a founder's guide to avoiding common pitfalls, move through practical tools for safe development, explore workflow optimization, examine new product management approaches, and end with a reality check on AI's current limitations.

First, Tomasz Karwatka argues most founders are wasting the AI wave by chasing big, general products. The real opportunity lies in niche software. AI now lets you build specialized tools for specific industries much faster and cheaper than before. For founders in pharma tech or AI workflow platforms, this means identifying a domain you understand deeply and using AI as an accelerator, not the product itself. The key takeaway: don't build another chatbot. Build something that solves a real problem for a specific group of people who will pay for it.

Second, Stanford's Jai tool addresses a critical safety concern. AI agents with full filesystem access can accidentally delete or modify important files. Jai creates a lightweight sandbox on Linux to run AI agents safely. It is free, easy to use, and reduces harm while not being a perfect security solution. For developers building AI-powered applications, this tool provides immediate protection against costly mistakes. Implement Jai in your development workflow today to add a crucial safety layer.

Third, Avi Chawla explains the anatomy of the .claude folder. This hidden directory controls how Claude behaves in your projects through files like CLAUDE.md. These files store instructions, commands, skills, agents, and permissions. Understanding this system helps you customize Claude for team workflows or personal knowledge management. For anyone using AI assistants regularly, organizing these files saves time and improves consistency. Start by creating a basic CLAUDE.md file with your most common instructions.

Fourth, Cat Wu describes product management on the AI exponential. With models improving rapidly, traditional long-term planning becomes obsolete. Successful teams now experiment quickly, demo often, and iterate with each new model release. This approach treats AI as a creative partner rather than just a tool. For product managers in AI companies, the lesson is to embrace rapid experimentation over detailed roadmaps. Build features that can evolve alongside the underlying technology.

Finally, Patricia Fernández de Lis examines AI's attempt to compete in math olympiads. While models claimed performance matching top students, they did not truly compete under human conditions. The contest requires pencil-and-paper problem solving without external help. Experts suggest AI should have separate contests, preserving the olympiad as a test of pure human thought. This reminds us that AI excels at different tasks than humans. When building AI products, focus on what machines do well rather than trying to replicate human cognition.

Together, these articles paint a complete picture: identify niche opportunities, build safely, optimize workflows, manage products adaptively, and understand AI's true capabilities. The common thread is practical application over theoretical hype.
